The ideal job

The job: CIO in the Public Service

What is the role really?

My ideal job is as a Chief Information Officer (CIO) or Chief Technology Officer (CTO) for one of the Public Service agencies in Australia. As a CIO or CTO you work with your team to build out the strategy around technology for the agency, aligned to the vision that the agency has for its future. This requires a breadth of knowledge across technology, as well as an understanding from a business perspective of how you can support the delivering of business vision through technical innovation. The role has equal parts business leadership and management along with technical service delivery. I appreciate that specifying that I want it to be in the Public Sector sounds odd, but there are a number of reasons for this:
  • I started my working life in the Army and have a passion for public service. Service is part of my DNA and finding ways to support the growth of our economy and success of our country are things I am passionate about.
  • The public sector is one area that still has opportunities for real transformational change in the technology space. Many agencies are carrying a huge amount of technical debt - old infrastructure and on-premises assets that they need to migrate from if they want to make use of the opportunities presented by the cloud. For many agencies, they are still only just scraping the surface of what could be possible.
  • I love a challenge. I feel that one of my key skills is finding a way through organisational complexities and there are few organisations as complex to navigate as those of the public service.

What skills, qualifications and experience is required for the role?

Each job description for CIO or CTO varies, depending on the agency or organisation, but common to most them tend to be the following:
  • A relevant degree - Computer Science or Engineering tend to be preferred.
  • Technical expertise, with depth of knowledge in areas such as security, cyber security and cloud services.
  • Experience with executing transformational ICT programs and leading IT teams.
  • In-depth knowledge of current and emerging IT trends.
  • Strategic thinking and the ability to be pragmatic and analytical.
  • The ability to develop creative solutions to complex problems and business requirements.
  • Experience in leading continuous improvement programs and change management activities.
  • Knowledge of how to build a performance-based culture which is aligned to strategic objectives (rewarding the right behaviours to drive the required outcomes).
    •  
  • Ability to communicate effectively and work collaboratively internally and externally, as a partner, negotiator or influencer.

Project concept

Hire me please - an Overview

How often have you read a job description and then failed to apply for the role because you didn’t think you met one of the listed criteria? There is an astonishing amount of research in this space around self-screening. An often quoted piece of research by Hewlett Packard found that women will not apply for a role unless they believe they meet 100% of the listed criteria, whereas men will apply when they meet around 60%. This means that employers are missing large parts of their target market through the Inclusive hiring in the IT industry is a huge focus for employers at the moment, and the first real window into any organisation is their job description. This is the point at which companies, sometimes unknowingly, are turning off potential future employees. What they say, and how they say it is critical in the fight for talent. Hire me please offers employers a tool to help them quickly and easily build inclusive job descriptions, optimised to attract the broadest range of potential candidates to their organisation. Using an automated interface, users simply select from pre-defined fields and the tool builds an inclusive job description, based around current best practice, removing as much bias as possible from the process and reducing the load on managers, HR and talent acquisition teams. Hire me please uses industry standards, organisational preferences and the data gathered through the application process to further refine and learn, in order to remain relevant over time.

Motivation

Over the last five years I have witnessed an increasing focus on attracting women into technical roles. Precise statistics in this space are hard to come by due to the way in which employers report on their staff. A 2015 report by the National Centre for Women & IT had women as 25% of the technical work force. I would suggest in many cases, it is much lower than this as women take up roles in service delivery, project management and leadership, which can often get grouped up by IT employers as 'technical'. In my own organisation, across Asia, our female technical talent accounts for just 7% of our employees. If we look at other areas of inclusive hiring such as neurodiversity, the statistics are even less reliable as they require self-identification and very often there is still considered to be a stigma attached. So why does this really matter? Well, according to McKinsey, gender diverse companies are 15% more likely to have a financial performance above the industry mean, with the implication being that building diversity into an organisation can become a competitive differentiator. So why isn’t every organisation simply making sure that their workforce is diverse? Well, of course, it isn’t quite as simple as that. Gender diverse companies are 15% more likely to have a financial performance above the industry mean. (McKinsey 2015) In a current study I am running around inclusive hiring, 38% of respondents say that the number one reason they don’t apply for a job is that they thought they didn’t meet the listed criteria. The research shows that there is a large portion of the addressable market who are self screening, based on what they are reading in a job description and the Hewlett Packard research reinforces this. The simplest change any organisation can make in terms of improving their talent pipeline is through tweaking their job descriptions. The challenge here is knowing what to change, what really matters and how to build all of that into a compelling, engaging and realistic job description. Most managers find themselves time poor and the recruitment function in many organisations is stretched across multiple roles, offshored and using a company standard that has existed for year. The simple act of including a non-essential degree requirement, or specifying that the job requires extensive travel, when in reality it is well planned and entirely manageable are just two examples of the kinds of things organisations add to their job requirements every day – without realising the proportion of candidates they have excluded through those simple actions. Building an application to enable users to rapidly build highly inclusive job descriptions will immediately increase the number of candidates applying for roles, and give organisations greater diversity to choose from.

Description

On opening the application, users will have options they can select to determine the type of role they are recruiting for, and any critical parameters that have been set by the organisation (level, tenure, full time, part time, language). The user will then be provided with a series of variables they can select from including:
  • the kind of level of experience required for the role – graduate, entry level, senior, people manager
  • any qualifications, certifications or accreditations that are really essential in order to perform the job,
  • travel requirements
  • security clearances
Having made their selections, the application will draw upon a pre-defined repository of content and build a new job description for the end user. The majority of this content will be pre-populated into an application library by the organisation and can include any content they already use. The application will present the draft version of the job description in the interface to enable review and any additions to be made. Users can made edits directly into the draft through the interface. Once the user is happy with their draft, they will submit it. The application will then run an assessment on the job description, providing it a mark out of 100 as to its inclusivity, and making any recommendations for changes that could be made to improve it. The tool will also provide the user with statistics around the potentially benefits of particular changes. For example, the application could tell the user that based on research, if they were to remove a degree requirement from the job description, applications would increase by 30%. This enables users to make data informed decisions around what to include or not include in their final version. Recommendations that are accepted by the users will be fed back into the repository to enable the tool to iterate. For each future job description that meets the same or similar criteria in the initial screen, the application can make suggestions or show the most recent similar role that it produced. With a link into the HR or recruitment tooling, the application can also create dashboards to show which job descriptions are being the most successful. This form of gamification encourages users to really focus on the content they are developing, as well as identifying successful starting points.

Tools and technologies

The application and interface could be built in a number of ways. The two main options would be: 1. Using pre-existing SaaS products that are configured to support the requirements. This could be done using a PowerApps front end and user interface which then leverages Flow, Forms, a SharePoint repository, SQL databases and other Office suite tools to provide an output to an end user. The Machine Learning component of the tool could potentially be built using some of the out of the box connectors and capability in Azure, but would likely still need configuration from a Data Scientist to develop any custom algorithms required. Limitations: The limitation of this approach is that it relies on all organisations who will want to use it to have licensing for the Microsoft tooling. It also limits customisation as the tooling lends itself more to configuration, rather than customisation. Benefits: This approach will potentially reduce complexity and therefore cost as all the base coding is done and the work required will be a matter of configuration – creating the necessary connections between repositories, setting up a UI and leveraging built in platform capabilities. 2. Building the application from the ground up in a platform agnostic way. This approach would require a much larger code base to be developed from scratch, but would also allow for more personalisation and flexibility. Leveraging an OSS code base might help to fast track this, so a combination of software such as Git, GitHub, Terraform/Azure DevOps and the preferred code base tooling, depending on the developer will be critical. Some kind of data storage will be required, depending on the Machine Learning capabilities that are built in. This in turn will need to be hosted and during the application development an approach will have to be agreed as to where the ML capability resides – whether it draws upon every job description being created, or whether it is dedicated to a singular organisation. Limitations: The main challenge with this approach is that it will potentially be more time intensive. Having to built everything from the ground up will potentially take longer than configuring pre-existing tools. Benefits: A lot more flexibility in the end product as it can be built exactly to specifications and can pull data from a lot more different sources.

Skills required

The skills required for this project are not particularly rare or hard to find. The majority of the work is standard application development, depending on the approach taken. The following are the main skills that will be required:
  • Application developer
  • UX designer
  • Data engineer to support the data connections that will be needed
  • Data scientist in order to build the necessary algorithms to enable the app to learn and iterate.
The only one of these that tends to be more specialist is the Data Scientist. If Hire Me Please is going to remain relevant, it needs to be able to learn from previous interactions so it can continue to build up to date and highly inclusive job descriptions.

Outcome

Using a combination of organisational requirements, in concert with current research in the field of inclusion, employers will have at their finger tips a tool that can create custom job descriptions, optimised to attract diverse talent to the organisation. Hire Me Please aims to take the guess work out of inclusive hiring and assist in creating a consistent hiring experience for candidates, whether the organisation is 10 people or 100,000 people. By solving the challenges inherent in creating an unbiased job description, Hire Me Please will help organisations add greater diversity to their workforce, and therefore increase their potential for profitability.
